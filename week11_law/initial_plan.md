# This is the initial research plan

### Key Points on Official Documents for Generative AI Regulation, Ethics, and Policy
- **Hong Kong**: Research indicates a primary focus on voluntary guidelines and privacy laws, with the Generative AI Technical and Application Guideline as the core document for ethical practices, supplemented by the Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance for data handling.
- **Europe (EU)**: Evidence points to the AI Act as the central binding regulation, establishing harmonized rules that emphasize risk-based approaches, transparency, and ethical obligations for generative AI systems.
- **United States**: It appears policies center on executive actions promoting innovation, with recent orders revoking barriers and the non-binding Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights guiding ethical use, alongside state-level laws like Colorado's for consumer protections.
- **United Kingdom**: Approaches suggest a principles-based framework via white papers and action plans, fostering pro-innovation ethics without mandatory laws, with documents like the AI Opportunities Action Plan and AI Playbook providing practical guidance.

#### Overview of Approaches
These regions prioritize balancing innovation with ethical safeguards in official documents, though enforcement varies—EU mandates are stringent, while others favor flexibility. Common themes include bias mitigation, data privacy, and accountability, but complexities arise in implementation, such as adapting to rapid AI advancements.

#### Ethical and Policy Focus
Documents universally address fairness, transparency, and risk management, with hedging for uncertainties like emerging harms from generative models. For instance, guidelines often recommend human oversight and audits, acknowledging debates on over- vs. under-regulation.

### Hong Kong
Hong Kong's approach emphasizes voluntary, practical guidelines integrated with existing privacy laws, promoting ethical AI adoption without comprehensive binding legislation. The framework balances innovation with risk mitigation, focusing on privacy, reliability, and security in generative AI contexts.

- **Hong Kong Generative Artificial Intelligence Technical and Application Guideline**  
  Issuing Authority: Digital Policy Office, Hong Kong Government  
  Publication Date: April 2025  
  Description: This guideline provides codes and practices for generative AI, covering technical background, scope, limitations, potential risks (e.g., hallucinations, biases, data poisoning), governance principles (e.g., privacy, IP respect, reliability), and operational recommendations for developers, providers, and users. It classifies risks as unacceptable, high, limited, or low, and suggests tools like watermarks and audits to ensure ethical deployment.  
  Official Link: [PDF](https://www.digitalpolicy.gov.hk/en/our_work/data_governance/policies_standards/ethical_ai_framework/doc/HK_Generative_AI_Technical_and_Application_Guideline_en.pdf)  
  Additional Context: Released to boost safe adoption, it aligns with broader ethical AI frameworks and includes sector-specific advice, such as for finance via the Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA).

- **Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance (Cap. 486)**  
  Issuing Authority: Hong Kong e-Legislation, Department of Justice  
  Publication Date: 1995 (effective 1996, with amendments up to 2022)  
  Description: This ordinance protects individuals' privacy rights regarding personal data, which is integral to generative AI ethics by regulating data collection, use, transfer, and erasure. It includes six data protection principles (e.g., purpose limitation, accuracy, security) and prohibitions on cross-border data transfers without safeguards, addressing risks like privacy breaches in AI training data.  
  Official Link: [Full Text](https://www.elegislation.gov.hk/hk/cap486)  
  PDF: [Contents and Full Ordinance](https://www.elegislation.gov.hk/hk/cap486%21en.pdf)  
  Additional Context: Applies to generative AI through requirements for data minimization and consent, with enforcement by the Privacy Commissioner for Personal Data (PCPD), including recent 2025 guidance on AI governance.

### Europe (EU)
The EU adopts a rigorous, risk-based regulatory framework, with the AI Act as the cornerstone. It classifies generative AI under general-purpose AI (GPAI) models, imposing mandatory obligations for transparency, risk assessments, and ethical compliance, while banning unacceptable practices like manipulative techniques.

- **Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 – Laying Down Harmonised Rules on Artificial Intelligence (AI Act)**  
  Issuing Authority: European Parliament and Council, via EUR-Lex  
  Publication Date: June 13, 2024 (entered into force August 1, 2024; phased implementation through 2025)  
  Description: This regulation establishes harmonized rules for AI, including generative systems, with risk classifications (unacceptable, high, limited, minimal). For GPAI, it requires technical documentation, copyright compliance, bias mitigation, explainability, and societal impact assessments. Ethical focuses include human rights alignment, privacy under GDPR, and prohibitions on emotion recognition in sensitive contexts. Systemic risks apply to models exceeding 10^25 FLOPs.  
  Official Link: [Full Text](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng)  
  PDF: [Download](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/PDF/?uri=OJ:L_202401689)  
  Additional Context: Supported by the European AI Office for enforcement, with fines up to 7% of global turnover; 2025 updates include GPAI guidelines for ethical innovation.

### United States
The US features a decentralized, innovation-oriented model with federal executive orders and non-binding ethical guidelines, complemented by state laws. Recent 2025 actions emphasize deregulation to foster competitiveness, while addressing ethical concerns like equity and privacy.

- **Executive Order: Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence**  
  Issuing Authority: The White House  
  Publication Date: January 23, 2025  
  Description: This order revokes prior AI directives seen as barriers, promoting innovation by eliminating ideological biases and enhancing national security. It directs agencies to prioritize economic competitiveness in AI policies, including generative technologies, while encouraging ethical self-regulation.  
  Official Link: [Full Text](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/01/removing-barriers-to-american-leadership-in-artificial-intelligence/)  
  Additional Context: Part of broader 2025 reforms, it includes an AI Action Plan due mid-2025, focusing on voluntary standards for harms like misinformation.

- **Executive Order: Preventing Woke AI in the Federal Government**  
  Issuing Authority: The White House  
  Publication Date: July 23, 2025  
  Description: Building on prior orders, it aims to ensure trustworthy AI in federal use, addressing ethical risks like biases while promoting innovation. It revokes elements of earlier policies to reduce restrictions on generative AI deployment.  
  Official Link: [Full Text](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/07/preventing-woke-ai-in-the-federal-government/)  
  Additional Context: Aligns with America's AI Action Plan, identifying over 90 policy actions for innovation and infrastructure.

- **America's AI Action Plan**  
  Issuing Authority: The White House  
  Publication Date: July 10, 2025  
  Description: This plan outlines federal actions across pillars like accelerating innovation and building AI infrastructure, with ethical considerations for generative AI in national security and public welfare. It prioritizes intelligence on foreign AI projects and revokes burdensome regulations.  
  Official Link: [PDF](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Americas-AI-Action-Plan.pdf)  
  Additional Context: Emphasizes collaboration and risk management, including for biases and privacy.

- **Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights: Making Automated Systems Work for the American People**  
  Issuing Authority: White House Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP)  
  Publication Date: October 2022  
  Description: This non-binding framework advocates for safe systems, privacy protections, equity, and bias prevention in AI, including generative tools. It includes principles like algorithmic discrimination protections and data privacy, serving as ethical guidance for federal and state policies.  
  Official Link: [Full Text](https://www.whitehouse.gov/ostp/ai-bill-of-rights/)  
  PDF: [Download](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/Blueprint-for-an-AI-Bill-of-Rights.pdf)  
  Additional Context: Referenced in 2023-2025 memos for AI governance and risk management.

- **Colorado Senate Bill 24-205: Consumer Protections for Artificial Intelligence (Colorado AI Act)**  
  Issuing Authority: Colorado General Assembly  
  Publication Date: 2024 (enacted)  
  Description: This state law requires developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems, including generative ones, to conduct impact assessments, mitigate algorithmic discrimination, and provide transparency. It focuses on ethical issues like bias in decisions affecting consumers.  
  Official Link: [Bill Text](http://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205)  
  PDF (Enrolled Version): [Download](https://leg.colorado.gov/sites/default/files/documents/2024A/bills/2024a_205_enr.pdf)  
  Additional Context: Prohibits discrimination in areas like employment and healthcare; part of fragmented US state initiatives.

### United Kingdom
The UK employs a pro-innovation, sector-led strategy with non-statutory guidance, emphasizing principles like safety and accountability. Documents promote ethical AI through voluntary codes, with plans for potential legislation in 2025.

- **A Pro-Innovation Approach to AI Regulation (White Paper)**  
  Issuing Authority: Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT), UK Government  
  Publication Date: March 29, 2023 (updated August 3, 2023)  
  Description: This white paper outlines a proportionate framework for AI regulation, including generative AI, based on five cross-sectoral principles (safety, transparency, fairness, accountability, contestability). It encourages ethical innovation, international interoperability, and sector-specific guidance.  
  Official Link: [Full Text](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-regulation-a-pro-innovation-approach/white-paper)  
  PDF (Government Response): [Download](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/65c1e399c43191000d1a45f4/a-pro-innovation-approach-to-ai-regulation-amended-governement-response-web-ready.pdf)  
  Additional Context: Sets vision for regulators; updated with 2024 guidance on implementing principles.

- **AI Opportunities Action Plan**  
  Issuing Authority: Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT), UK Government  
  Publication Date: January 13, 2025  
  Description: This plan provides a roadmap to capture AI opportunities for growth and public benefits, including ethical foundations like infrastructure, data unlocking, talent development, and safe development. It addresses generative AI through actions like AI Safety Institute growth, regulatory reforms, and public-private partnerships for national champions. Key sections cover building AI infrastructure (e.g., 10-year plan, AI Growth Zones), data assets (e.g., National Data Library), talent strategies (e.g., scholarships, immigration enhancements), and responsible scaling (e.g., "Scan > Pilot > Scale" for adoption). Ethical emphases include bias mitigation, human oversight, environmental sustainability, and societal wellbeing.  
  Official Link: [Full Text](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-opportunities-action-plan/ai-opportunities-action-plan)  
  PDF (Government Response): [Download](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/678639913a9388161c5d2376/ai_opportunities_action_plan_government_repsonse.pdf)  
  Additional Context: Foreword highlights UK strengths; conclusion stresses long-term commitment for transformational benefits.

- **Artificial Intelligence Playbook for the UK Government**  
  Issuing Authority: Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT), UK Government  
  Publication Date: February 10, 2025  
  Description: This playbook expands on the Generative AI Framework, offering guidance for public sector AI use, including 10 principles (e.g., lawful/ethical use, human control, security). It covers understanding AI fields (e.g., generative, agentic), building solutions (e.g., teams, user research), buying AI (e.g., procurement), and safe/responsible deployment (e.g., ethics, data protection, governance). Ethical focuses include fairness, accountability, contestability, societal wellbeing, and risk management throughout the lifecycle.  
  Official Link: [Full Text (HTML)](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-playbook-for-the-uk-government/artificial-intelligence-playbook-for-the-uk-government-html)  
  Additional Context: Includes appendices with case studies (e.g., GOV.UK Chat, NHS tools); emphasizes collaboration and quality assurance.
